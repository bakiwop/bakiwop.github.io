I've been wanting to do a journal kinda thing for a while now, but I didn't want to use a CMS, they usually end up breaking (and when that happens, it can be a pain in the butt to fix, or, you lose all your data) and there isn't as much control over the layout (not that this layout is the layout to end all layouts, but I know what most of it does since I coded it myself, and changes are pretty easy). Except I'll be writing with electrons, very 21st century-y, dontcha think?

And all this so that in twenty years I can ask myself, "Hey when exactly was that big blow we had on the coast? You know, when the power didn't actually go out" and then I can scour the archives and find what I'm looking for..

Exciting stuff.

The Winter of My Discontent
#ope

I have a high tolerance for boredom. Always have had. On those exceedingly infrequent occasions when I begin to feel boredom crawling across my brain, moving like a marine layer off the ocean insinuating itself into civilization, creeping amongst the narrow winding roads and cedar-sided cabins on the headlands and promontories bumping up to the sea, well, I know I'm in for a couple hours of happy fun time.

Discontentment.

It usually starts with me wishing I was somewhere else. Oftentimes the Twin Cities, but sometimes its Chicago or New York or London. I've lived in the same town for nearly nine years. Nine years! My wife and I have been together almost 29 years, and in those 29 years we've lived in 23 different places, so to live somewhere for nine years when you're used to moving, on average, more than once a year?

And then I start thinking about the fact that I have at least 16 more years until official retirement age...and...I picture myself sitting at this same reference desk in this same book mine for sixteen more years. Sixteen! And that's followed immediately by two more thoughts.

1) That'll be what happens if I'm lucky.

2) The retirement age will probably be moved back to 70 or 75 by then.

So, as I'm looking at the absurd price of rentals in my ville du jour, I begin my best Judy Garland impression, reciting the magic words, "There's no place like home," over and again until I believe the truth of the matter: I'm very fortunate. I have an amazing wife. We have a nice house. We live in a crazy beautiful part of the world. I get paid fairly well to help people. Every day. And I don't have to say to them, 'that'll be $39.99...cash or credit?' when I'm done helping them. I simply get to wish them a good rest of the day and let them get on with their life.

It's good to experience discontentment. Occasionally, anyway. It helps you evaluate your life. It helps remind you of your good fortune.

I still wish I were living in the Cities, though.

Billionaires
#mattifesto

Bernie Sanders says income over $1bn should be taxed at 100%

It isn't the income that's a problem. Billionaires don't make billions in income, they have assets - stocks in companies, other financial assets and instruments, and goods like art or oil. They borrow against these and service the interest.

For instance, the CEO of a corporation (or corporations, brief aside - what exactly does it say about the value a CEO brings to a company when a person can be the CEO of multiple corporations?) can take loans against these assets to buy lots of yachts and mansions and cars and influence...oh so much influence.

What's needed is a tax on the loans they take out. Or even better, on their ability to take out those kinds of loans in the first place. Or best, make setting up a corporation in that way illegal. Force corporations to be structured in such a way that no one person becomes so wildly rich they can launch a car into space on a whim.

There shouldn't be billionaires. There shouldn't be hundred millionaires. Economic inequality should not be so vast. And frankly? That's just good business sense. The more money folk have the more they can purchase and invest, the more they can purchase and invest the better the economy does.

2024.12.27
#ope

I had nightmares last night. First time in I don't even know how long.

Yesterday was a bad day. It would have been my parents' 53rd wedding anniversary. My father died this year.

I wasn't expecting... well... I wasn't expecting it to be this hard this time of year. Then I thought it through: his birthday was on the 3rd of November; Thanksgiving on the 28th; Advent, and boy did my dad love Advent (my dad was a Roman Catholic priest, ordained in 1960, and, because of quirks of the church, he remained a priest for the rest of his life); Christmas on the 25th; my parents anniversary on the 26th; my wife's and my anniversary on the 30th; New Year's; their only grandchild's birthday on January 14th; and my mom's birthday on the 19th.

When I'm at work in the book mines, I sit at a reference desk, in the middle of the library, for eight hours a day. It's the place in the library I like being best. Being available to patrons all the time is groovy. Except sometimes it isn't. Being a reference librarian is, essentially, a customer service position. The customers are called patrons and I don't have to ask them for money when I serve them, but it's a customer service position.

Most of us have had customer service jobs at one point or other in our life. Imagine yourself at that job, then imagine yourself sitting in the middle of the customers for eight hours a day for fourteen years. It can be stressful sometimes, and, it turns out, when you're unknowingly mourning your dead father is one of those times.

So I'm sitting at the reference desk yesterday - thank goodness it was a slow day - and I start tearing up occasionally - and realize I've been thinking about dad.

Specifically? I was born and raised in Wisconsin, and went to college in (at least, one of the colleges I ended up at was in) the Twin Cities. And after college I moved up there multiple times for jobs. I had girlfriends in Minnesota. I met my wife in St. Paul. Anyway, lots of my life took place there, and it was a five(ish) hour drive between the Cities and home. The number of times my parents took me up there and back - be it for college, or a job, or to help me move to a new apartment was... we'd sometimes end up at a Country Kitchen restaurant in Tomah. It was somewhere around the halfway mark of the trip. Eating out was always a special occasion. It didn't happen much.

It occurred to me that I might never make that trip again. I might end up actually living in the Twin Cities again because my wife and I love it there, but that five hour trip to Wisconsin wouldn't be a thing anymore. That trip was such a big part of my life for so long. That Tomah stop was such a big part of my life... but my dad is dead and my mom is getting sick of the snow and cold, so even if I were to live in the Twin Cities again, there'd be no reason to make the trip to Wisconsin.

There'd be no family in Wisconsin. No family in the little Wisconsin town I grew up in. We have tons of relatives in Chicago, but nobody in Wisconsin anymore.

No more family drive. No more dad helping me load or unload a U-Haul. No more dad.

No more dad.

And there's nothing to be done about it. No way to fix it.

We had a cat. Snowflake. She also died this year.

So that didn't help with yesterday either.

We never thought of her as a fur baby and we were not her mom and dad, but she absolutely was a part of our family. We loved her. She loved us.

There's a thing with cats sometimes. Even though there's nothing wrong and there are people they love and trust with them they start getting anxious. It doesn't matter if you try to pick them up to calm them down, they just keep getting more anxious and they start looking around for escape routes and they aren't registering much that's happening around them and they start shaking. I think they feel trapped. Then, when you let them go they bolt away. Sometimes they snap out of it right away and other times they go for a lie down in the sun or in a dark, quiet room. Decompressing.

That's how I felt yesterday. I could feel that anxious energy building up in me all afternoon. There was nothing to be done about it. There was nowhere I could go to escape it. I had all these staff members and patrons around me needing help, and because it's a customer service position you smile and joke around, and I kept thinking about my dad. I felt trapped.

I made it through to the end of the day. Hugged my wife when she came to pick me up. Spent the entire evening decompressing. Had nightmares. Woke up this morning feeling... what... less anxious? Annoyed by the nightmares?

I don't feel trapped today, though I am feeling a certain inevitability and confusion of it all. Life, that is.
